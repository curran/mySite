Engaged Living
1/15/2005

“Someone within 200 feet of us right now will probably win the Nobel Prize within the next five years,” said Bill Theurkauf. We were standing in his biotech lab where I have been a research assistant since June. He was referring to scientists in our building who conducted founding research on RNAi; a powerful gene silencing tool. Hearing these words, I was in disbelief that I was so close to such accomplished people. This situation is one of many that have made me realize that everything in this world is really there, and the potential for me to get involved is also there. It is difficult to say exactly when or why this realization occurred, but since that time and more so with every day, I am living in a more engaged way.

I had no reason to be engaged until I discovered music; it changed my life! I finally found something that genuinely captured my interest. Music forced me to think in an organized manner and has been an extraordinary catalyst for my evolution as a person. What has fascinated me in music is the physics of it and its logical depth. This complicated thing can be easily perceived and interpreted by the human brain and even be a medium through which emotions are communicated. The further you go in trying to understand music, the more music becomes science. This has led me to shift my efforts to pursue science with the same enthusiasm and inspiration I had when I first discovered music.

I developed serious interest in science during my junior year biology class. I took an opportunity to volunteer as a research assistant at UMass Medical School that summer. We are currently working on understanding specific proteins involved with RNAi, objectively probing the inner workings of nature. The more exposure I have to science, with its profound relevance and fascinating complexity, the more I want immerse myself in it. This desire is the dark energy that is accelerating my universe outward.

My experiences have led me to the realization that I have the ability to self-learn. I first applied this to my music studies and guitar performance, then later to math, science, and academics. Music is a gift I possess and can further develop at any time. Science requires building on layers of understanding that can only be achieved through progressive instruction and continued self-learning. My immediate goal is to pursue science through formal education as well as independent study.

My ultimate goal is to realize my full potential as a human being, no matter how hard I must work, and no matter the sacrifices. At this point in my life, I must learn everything I possibly can. In the future I hope to return what I have learned to the world in an effort to better humankind. My world is one filled with opportunity and support, for that I am grateful. This world has not only shaped my dreams and aspirations, but has allowed me to become engaged.

*I was rejected by MIT
*the person which Bill Theurkauf referred to, Craig C. Mello, actually did win the Nobel prize in 2006 for discovering RNAi
