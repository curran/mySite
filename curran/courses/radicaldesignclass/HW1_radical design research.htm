Commercialism and Radical Design

The first things that I came across in my radical design search were several businesses which called themselves "Radical design", which I find comical.

These include:

1        "Radical Design" - A company based in Holland which designs and sells "recumbent bags, bicycle trailers and dog packs.” (1)

o This area of bags and portability of things is a perfect candidate for radical new designs, which is apparently the basis if this company.

o On another site (somebody’s blog), I found a good description of one of their products:

"it looks like the Radical trailer effectively becomes a large duffle

bag when not in use as a trailer, with the addition of a wheel bag

rather inelegantly tacked onto the top. The remainder of the trailer

fits into the bag ..." (2)

This is a novel idea, and a good idea, but I don’t know if it is really radical.

2        "Radical Designs" - A web design and setup business. (3)

3        “Free Radical Design” – a small gaming development business. (4)

 

I think a big reason why several companies used “radical design” in their name is because they want their name to sound awesome. This gives some clue as to the relationship between radical design and commercialism/marketing, which I found to be a recurring theme. People are probably generally more interested in something that is radical as opposed to something that is conventional. I have a feeling that this fact will impact the marketing aspect of a “radical” product tremendously.

I came across an article that talked about a case where a company is pushing the idea of radical design for the purpose of marketing (5) (perhaps not the only reason, but a big one). DesignWorks is a company that is pushing radical design applied to the design of aircraft interiors and exteriors. The article conveyed to me that radical design plays a tremendous role in the marketing of the product, which is one reason why they favor it. This is fairly obvious and makes sense, but I wonder how much a radical design actually changes the approach to marketing it.

Take the terms “coolness”, “quality”, and “usefulness”. These are all interrelated and all important to the success of something, but at the same time you could determine each one individually. I think that “radicalness” is the same way. Therefore the correlation between radical design and marketing is not the whole story, but a part of it.

I would imagine that if something is radical enough (AND has the necessary qualifications for success), it is an advertisement for itself, and thus marketing would not really be as necessary, or may not be necessary at all! For example; Dean Kamen's invention the Segway Human Transporter - an electric scooter for use in pedestrian areas. The Segway I would say is “radically” different from conventional scooters, and I think this is a major factor to it’s success. I have not seen many ads for the Segway, yet it is still a pretty widespread and successful product.

Radical Design in Science and Engineering

Engineering and the sciences are areas in which I would think radical design would be promoted heavily, and thus be implemented more often there as compared to other arenas. Often the goal of science and engineering is to do something a new way or discover something novel. Although just because something is new or novel does not mean that it is radical, it is already closer to being radical than most things.

Synthetic Biology

One striking example of what I see as Radical design in science is synthetic biology. “Biologists are crafting libraries of interchangeable DNA parts and assembling them inside microbes to create programmable, living machines.” (6) Scientists at MIT (namely Drew Endy) have created a global repository for “BioBricks,” engineered genetic (DNA) parts with specific functions (logic gates for example) that can be used with other BioBricks to form components which can be combined to form an entire biological system engineered from the ground up. If this is viewed as an approach to understanding biology, it is so different from the conventional approach (where biologists mess with something to see what is does) that I would say it is radical. They are building it themselves instead of chipping various pieces away to see what happens.

Another aspect of their approach is that they are applying old concepts from engineering and software development such as modularity, re-usability, black-box-like information hiding and abstraction (which sounds a lot like object oriented design) to engineering in the biological domain. Essentially what they are doing is taking two pre-existing things; genetic engineering and object oriented design; and putting them together in a revolutionary fashion. This is not a new idea, people have wanted to do it for a long time. It is happening only now because the necessary technology just recently came into maturity. Is what they are doing radical?

Aerodynamic Topology Optimization and Evolutionary Computation

In the reverse direction, people are also applying biologically based concepts to the engineering domain. Windo Hutabarat from The University of Cambridge is applying evolutionary computation to aerodynamic topology optimization. (7) What he is attempting to do is have a computer generate a bunch (a “generation”) of random 3D shapes (topologies) that are aerodynamically optimal, then have a computer program select the one that most closely matches a “goal” shape which is pre-defined. This process is repeated; a new “generation” of random topologies is generated, but the new shapes based on the previously selected shape. This virtual generation and selection process (“evolutionary computation” or “the genetic algorithm”) is repeated until a desirable shape is achieved.

At first glance, this approach seems radical to me, but I must admit I am ignorant of the conventional approaches to aerodynamic topology optimization, and also ignorant of the problem domains where evolutionary computation has already been used. Therefore cannot say with any validity whether this approach is radical or not.

Radical Design in Software

wiki

The notion of a web site that can be modified by anyone who views that page (wiki) I believe has the potential to be radical, depending on it’s application. Wiki sites are used by private groups; big and small, close or very far apart. This is not really radical because it is pretty much only an improvement on allowing certain people access to the web pages. Wiki is also used to facilitate huge "open" projects such as Wikipedia. I think this is a radical approach to developing something like an online encyclopedia.

 

How about Google? The way I understand Google is that they are sort of based on a fairly non-novel approach to making money: selling ads, but they execute it in an extreme way:

The conventional approach: Make most of your money by selling the goods (software), and sell advertising when you can and it doesn’t interfere with the goods.

Google’s approach: Make most of your money on advertising, and sell the goods (software) when you can and it doesn’t interfere with the advertising.

What I find semi-radical about Google is that they give away really incredible services and software for free. (such as: GMail, Google Picasa (free photo posting/“sharing”), Google Earth, Google Maps, etc.) I think they do this because once the free stuff is widely used, they can make just as much (or much more) profits from advertising than they would have if they had sold the services and software. What adds to the radicalness is the intelligence of the advertising. For example, Google displays ads that are relevant to the subject matter of the search or email.

Is Google radical? Perhaps. Does radicalness depend on the result or not? If not, then I would say that Google is not really radical, because it could be argued that they only do some things differently than other similar huge companies. If so, then I would say that Google is radical, because the results of their approach are really incredible. They are sort of making history in the sense that they are an extremely successful software company that is not founded on selling software! That’s radical, because the approach is so different from the norm.

Problems With Radical Design

            Radical designs by their very nature are more prone to problems than conventional ones. This is one main reason why conventional ideas would be preferred to radical ones. Only when conventional designs are inadequate do radical ones come to the fore. This means that the realistic implementation of radical design is more likely to be in solving problems that are unusually complex or difficult than in solving relatively simple problems. Here are two examples that illustrate this idea: the spork and the Transhab.

It is unlikely that a radical design will be used to solve a problem that has already been sufficiently solved, such as eating food. For example, the reasons why the success of the spork (spoon-fork) is limited is twofold: it is unnecessary in most situations to combine a spoon and fork, and with the spork, many of the essential qualities of the spoon and the fork are diminished greatly.

It is likely that a radical design will be used to solve a problem that has not yet been solved, such as how to construct living quarters for people to live in space. NASA in 1999 was debating which design to use for their Space Station Habitation Module (the living quarters for six astronauts). "NASA is still debating whether to build the International Space Station's Habitation Module using a more conservative design [a metal-shelled living module] or a radical design called the Transhab [an inflatable, multi-level living module]. But at the Habitation Module Commercialization Conference this week in Houston, both astronauts and NASA officials said they are leaning toward the latter."(8)

The Definition of Radical Design

How does one quantitatively measure the degree to which something is radical? Here is a progression of ideas that leads me to my most concise definition of it. (Keep in mind that I am making this all up and could be way off the mark.)

1        A) How many aspects of it are different from conventional designs that have been previously used in an attempt to solve the same problem?

2        B) How much risk does it involve?

o Risk of failure

§ Due to uselessness

·    Ex. The Spork.

§ Due to weaknesses

·    Ex. Wikipedia being destroyed by it’s users

o Risk of creating dangerous situations

§ Ex. Synthetic Biology, which will make bio-terrorism much easier.

3        C) How many new possibilities are made available by the design?

o Ex. Wikipedia allows for widely distributed development of one thing.

o Ex. Synthetic Biology (if it is successful) will allow an infinite number of new possibilities in the biological realm.

+ indicates unification of ideas, => indicates refinement

4        C+D => E) Does this new design enable dynamic situations that were not possible before?

o A distinguishing characteristic of radically designed systems is that they allow for really big scopes of new possibilities, which almost always includes undesirable ones. This is the root of both new possibilities and the risks associated with the design.

5        E => How large is the scope of new possibilities and dynamic situations that this design enables?

6        A+E => How large is the scope of new possibilities that this design enables as compared to that of pre-existing designs aimed to solve similar problems?

This is my end result. I still feel that this is only part of the full definition of Radical Design, but it is a start.

The Origins of Radical Design

            In all instances of things that I thought were radically designed, at the core of it, the design was taking two pre-existing concepts and putting them together in a new way. Is any “new” idea (or “design”) ever anything more than the extension or combination of existing ideas? There is “the old way” and there is “the new way”, but is there ever “the first way?” Something like the electric light bulb could be viewed as an improvement of the candle, yet it is still most definitely a radical design. It is radical because it was a new combination of things, and because it opened up an incredibly variety of possibilities both wide and deep. A place where “the first way” does exist is in discoveries. When a new way of doing something or using something is discovered, it most likely could be considered radical, but it cannot be someone’s design because it is a discovery, and previously unknown to mankind.

            This restricts “radical design” to combinations of pre-existing ideas (the only new ideas are that those combinations themselves). There could be a hierarchy of pre-existing idea combinations. This would mask the fact that pre-existing elements are the only constituent of the design, as well as make the design a greater leap forward than if it were a combination of only two ideas (perhaps more “radical?”).

Now the question is: what distinguishes an “improvement” from a “radical design?” Here is a proposed method for qualifying a “radical design”:

1.      How large is the hierarchy of pre-existing idea combinations that makes up this design?

2.      How large is the scope of new possibilities that this design enables as compared to that of pre-existing designs aimed to solve similar problems?

If the aforementioned two questions can be answered quantitatively, weighted properly, and combined, you would have a quantitative measurement of radicalness. Applying an appropriate heuristic threshold to this measurement will tell you whether or not something is a radical design.
