Affinity Diagrams

An affinity diagramming is a process or method by which many ideas or pieces of data are created and/or organized into categories or groups by a team of people. It is typically used for generating ideas to solve a particular problem.

            The process requires a team of people, preferably of four to six people who are each very experienced and knowledgeable in different areas. The team must first have a goal to reach or problem to solve and discuss the situation thoroughly so as to make sure everyone has a clear view of what needs to be done. Then there is a brainstorming or idea-generation period in which various ideas are discussed. During this period, everyone writes down ideas on post-it notes. Every idea goes on a separate note, and the notes must clearly express a whole concept (not just a single word for example). After a while, the post-it notes are posted on the wall in random places. Then there is a sorting phase, in which everyone moves the notes toward other cards that have ideas that may go together somehow, and organize them into groups. No one talks during this period, so as not to interfere with how other people move things. After a while of this, groups and subgroups become well defined. When groups can clearly be seen, header and subheader cards are created to give a concise title to the different groups and subgroups. After all of this, lines are drawn between headers to form the affinity diagram. The end result is a typical diagram of organization.

 

Cognitive Maps

            Cognitive maps are basically internal (in the mind) representations or models of outside things. These “maps” are usually built up from experience, but they can also be imparted by other humans.

            For example, after walking the same route every day, you get to know the route pretty well, including relative distances, landmarks, etc. This “knowing” is the cognitive map. After walking this route so many times, an internal representation of the route is built up and is pretty accurate. Based on landmarks, one can judge where they are on their internal map, and thus where they are in physical space.

            The ability to read maps (cartographic maps) is really the ability translate an abstract representation of physical space (the map you are reading) to an internal map (cognitive map) of physical space, which can then be directly compared to actual physical space.

            Cognitive maps are not limited to the special realm, they are used in all kinds of human activities. One kind of cognitive map could be a mental representation of how ideas and concepts are interrelated, a map of knowledge. A pictorial representation of these kinds of cognitive maps or any set of interrelated concepts is termed a concept map.

           

 

Cultural Probes

            Cultural probes are used to do ethnographic studies in a relatively non-invasive way. Traditionally, these kinds of studies are done by following someone of a group of people around and recording everything they do. This is pretty invasive, and does not work well with some kinds of people. The goal of cultural probes is to accomplish the same thing by providing the person(s) being studied means of self-documenting, then collecting that documentation after a period of time. For example, a cultural probe package may contain a camera, a notebook, a voice recorder, and other things that could be used to self-document. This has several main advantages; it makes the process less invasive, and it provides information about what the person being studied is thinking and feeling instead of only factual information.

            Cultural probing can be applied in the design process as a means to learn more about the people and environment at which your design is targeted. For example, in designing a computer support system to assist people who are disabled, elderly, or former psychiatric patients, conventional methods of ethnography such as direct observation of people is very invasive, and sometimes inappropriate. Cultural probes were used instead. (5)

            The idea of cultural probing can be used to not only learn about a person’s environment, but to let the probee make suggestions about what you are designing. This effectively distributes the design process, which may be very useful to any designer.

 

Behavior Sampling

            Behavior sampling is when behavior of a sample population is studied by means of observing the behavior of many single individuals. This is a common method in all behavioral studies, such as studies of animals, psychology, and neuroscience.

            Behavioral sampling is broken down into two methods: 1.) Focal-Animal sampling - individuals are monitored continuously for a period of time. 2.) Spot Sampling - sample the behavior of individuals at random times throughout the study. Spot sampling is used often in anthropological studies, and is the basis for most time allocation research (researching how much time is spent on a given task on the average). Focal-animal sampling is commonly used in animal behavioral studies.

            These kinds of methods can be used in the design process to see how your target population will likely respond to your design, or to study how they are behaving with relation to pre-existing designs related to your target.

 

Bodystorming

            Often in the design process, it is necessary to have intimate knowledge of how the user interacts with the product. A good way to learn about this is for the designers to actually put themselves into the user’s situation and be the user; really try it out. Bodystorming is a term for putting yourself in the user’s position and actually using the thing that you are designing. Bodystorming can also be thought of as carrying out the design process being in the position of the user.

            Bodystorming is useful because it gives the designers a much more intimate knowledge of the context of their product. This is absolutely necessary, otherwise design decisions may have critical flaws that were never even considered until it is too late. For example, one of the factors that contributes to design is how natural it feels to use a product. Bodystorming can be very effective at teaching the designers how “natural” or easy the product is to use.
